###Fataplus Business vision for 2024:








Creating a Minimal Viable Product (MVP) for an app like Fataplus, especially with a focus on supporting Malagasy farmers, involves identifying the core functionalities that address the primary needs of the users. Given the constraints of no-code platforms, the MVP will focus on simplicity, ease of use, and providing immediate value to the users. Below is a plan for developing an MVP using no-code tools, emphasizing critical features that can be quickly implemented and scaled based on user feedback.

 

MVP Objective:
To provide Malagasy farmers with an easy-to-use tool for managing farm tasks, tracking inventory, and accessing market prices and weather updates.

Core Features for MVP:
Task Management: Allow farmers to create, manage, and track daily farming tasks.

Inventory Tracking: Enable simple tracking of seeds, supplies, and harvested crops.

Market Prices: Provide access to current market prices for various crops.

Weather Updates: Offer daily weather forecasts relevant to the user's location.

No-Code Tools Selection:
App Builder: Adalo or Bubble for the app interface and logic. These platforms support the creation of custom databases for inventory and tasks, user authentication, and integrating external APIs for market prices and weather updates.

Database: Airtable or Google Sheets to manage user data, tasks, inventory records, and market prices. These can be easily integrated with the app built on Adalo or Bubble.

External APIs:

Weather API: OpenWeatherMap or similar for fetching weather forecasts.

Market Prices API: If a public API is unavailable, consider aggregating data into a Google Sheet or Airtable database periodically updated with market prices from reliable sources.

Development Steps:
Design the User Interface:

Use Adalo or Bubble to design a simple and intuitive interface focusing on the core features. Include navigation for tasks, inventory, market prices, and weather updates.

Set Up the Database:

Configure Airtable or Google Sheets to create tables for users, tasks, inventory items, and market prices. Design the schema to support easy updates and queries.

Integrate External APIs:

Connect the Weather API to fetch and display daily weather updates.

If available, integrate a Market Prices API to show current prices for various crops. Alternatively, manually update a database table with market prices at regular intervals.

Implement Core Features:

Task Management: Allow users to add, edit, and mark tasks as complete. Use the database to store and retrieve tasks.

Inventory Tracking: Enable users to add inventory items, specify quantities, and update records as items are used or harvested crops are added.

Market Prices and Weather Updates: Display the latest market prices and weather forecasts on the app's dashboard or relevant sections.

Testing and Feedback:

Conduct internal testing to ensure functionality works as expected. Then, release the MVP to a small group of users for feedback on usability, performance, and additional needs.

Launch and Iterate:

Launch the MVP publicly, encouraging user feedback. Continuously improve the app based on user suggestions and identified needs.

Post-MVP Considerations:
After the MVP phase, consider adding features like educational resources, a community forum for farmers, and enhanced analytics for market trends and farm performance. As the app grows, transitioning from no-code to custom development may be necessary to accommodate more complex features and scalability requirements.

This MVP plan leverages no-code tools to quickly bring a valuable product to Malagasy farmers, focusing on immediate needs while laying the groundwork for future enhancements and community building.

Understanding BFR (Besoin en Fonds de Roulement)

BFR represents the amount of cash a company needs to finance its operational cycle (production, storage, sales, and collection). It's the difference between operational current assets (stock, accounts receivable) and operational current liabilities (accounts payable).



Formula:

[ \text{BFR} = \text{Current Operational Assets} - \text{Current Operational Liabilities} ]



Steps to Estimate BFR in MGA:

Determine Current Operational Assets:



Stocks/Inventory (Raw Materials, Work in Progress, Finished Goods): Estimate the average value of stocks held during the year.

Accounts Receivable: Estimate the average amount of cash to be received from customers.



2. Determine Current Operational Liabilities:



Accounts Payable: Estimate the average amount of cash to be paid to suppliers.



3. Calculate BFR:



Subtract the Current Operational Liabilities from the Current Operational Assets.



Hypothetical Scenario:

Let's assume a small business in Madagascar involved in manufacturing artisanal goods. For simplicity, all values are hypothetical.



Stocks/Inventory: MGA 10,000,000 (raw materials + finished goods)

Accounts Receivable: MGA 5,000,000

Accounts Payable: MGA 4,000,000



[ \text{BFR} = (10,000,000 + 5,000,000) - 4,000,000 = 11,000,000 , \text{MGA} ]



Cost-Effectiveness Considerations:

Reducing BFR can improve a company's liquidity and reduce the need for external financing, which is cost-effective in the long term. Strategies might include:



Improving Inventory Turnover: Reducing the amount of stock held or increasing the speed of production.

Accelerating Receivables Collection: Implementing stricter credit terms or early payment incentives.

Delaying Payments to Suppliers: Negotiating longer payment terms without incurring penalties.



Conclusion:

While the above provides a conceptual framework for estimating BFR in MGA, the actual effectiveness and strategies to manage BFR cost-effectively will depend on the specific details of the business. It's also crucial to consider the impact of any strategy on business relationships and operational efficiency. For precise calculations and tailored financial advice, consulting with a financial professional is recommended.

Calculating an estimated Return on Investment (ROI) for a tech startup, particularly from the MVP phase through to a 3-year Go-to-Market strategy, involves several assumptions and projections. Let's create a hypothetical scenario for our Fataplus app, focusing on the Malagasy agricultural market. This example will outline potential revenues, ongoing expenses, and ultimately, an estimated ROI.

 

Assumptions:
Initial Investment: As previously calculated, the initial capital required to launch the MVP and cover the first three months of operation is approximately MGA 11,550,000.

Revenue Streams: Subscription fees from users (farmers, agribusinesses), advertising from agricultural product suppliers, and premium services (e.g., advanced analytics, personalized market insights).

Growth Rate: User base and revenue are projected to grow as the app gains traction. For simplicity, we'll assume a linear growth rate for both users and revenue over the 3 years.

Ongoing Expenses: Development, operational costs, marketing, and administrative expenses will continue post-launch.

Yearly Projections:
Year 1:
User Acquisition and Revenue Growth: Slow at the start, picking up towards the end of the year.

Revenue: Estimated at MGA 5,000,000 per month by the end of Year 1, totaling MGA 30,000,000 for the year (considering gradual growth over the year).

Net Profit/Loss for Year 1: MGA 30,000,000 (Revenue) - MGA 48,000,000 (Expenses) = -MGA 18,000,000.

Year 2:
Increased User Base and Revenue: Improved market presence and product maturity.

Revenue: Estimated at MGA 10,000,000 per month by the end of Year 2, totaling MGA 90,000,000 for the year.

Ongoing Expenses: Estimated at MGA 5,000,000 per month due to scaling, totaling MGA 60,000,000 for the year.

Net Profit/Loss for Year 3: MGA 150,000,000 (Revenue) - MGA 72,000,000 (Expenses) = MGA 78,000,000.

Calculating ROI:
To calculate ROI, we sum the net profits/losses for each year and divide by the total initial investment, then multiply by 100 to get a percentage.

[ \text{ROI} = \left( \frac{\text{Total Net Profit/Loss over 3 Years}}{\text{Total Initial Investment}} \right) \times 100 ]

[ \text{Total Net Profit/Loss over 3 Years} = -MGA 18,000,000 (\text{Year 1}) + MGA 30,000,000 (\text{Year 2}) + MGA 78,000,000 (\text{Year 3}) = MGA 90,000,000 ]

[ \text{ROI} = \left( \frac{MGA 90,000,000}{MGA 11,550,000} \right) \times 100 \approx 779% ]

Conclusion:
In this hypothetical scenario, the Fataplus app would achieve an estimated ROI of approximately 779% over three years from the start of the MVP into the Go-to-Market phase. This is a highly optimistic scenario, assuming steady growth in user base and revenue, and effective control of ongoing expenses. Real-world challenges, market dynamics, and unforeseen costs could significantly impact actual ROI. It's also important to continuously innovate and adapt to market needs to sustain growth and profitability.
